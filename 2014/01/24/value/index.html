Value

January 24, 2014

One of the difficult things about the software engineering world and especially of the web development world is trying to gage the value of a developer. We can’t be simply asked questions about our work, because it isn’t difficult to fake knowledge. We could check out people’s Github accounts, but that only works if the developer is able to work on work outside of work, or works at a company that allows work to be open sourced. The default is usually using methods like what degrees someone holds, how many years they’ve be developing, and other such arbitrary measures that don’t actually gage what a developers abilities or value.

As a young developer I feel as if I’ve encountered this problem. I’m only on my second year of “professional” development work. In reality I’m on my third because the work I did in college was just as professional as the work I’m doing now. In some ways it was more professional. Years are a bad judge because people can simply repeat their first two years of experience four times and claim 8 years of experience. That’s like a car company saying they’ve designed 200 million cars because they took 2 design and created 100 million cars from each.

Degrees offer value, the problem is education isn’t a practical form of skill training. This is a general misunderstanding of what education is. There is a thought that college is a way to prepare an individual for real life and a professional career. If this were true, we’d be sending plumbers and electricians to college and not trade school. I have a degree in English and Communication, but that didn’t teach me how to be a writer. I learned how to write from practical experience.

The web development industry needs to get a bit smarter with hiring practices. Companies should stop worrying about how flashy something is and start thinking big picture. A team of autodidacts and continual learners will provide not only higher quality work, but their work will also evolve over time. I don’t think a person can truly be an autodidact or consume knowledge without being able to evolve. Being a writer helps me write strong code, albeit sometimes messy code. As writers we never feel our work is finished, a story is never complete, a novel never finished, but we have to publish. Code to me is never complete. A project is never done. We ship a version but that isn’t a “we’re done”, it’s just another step along the path. I think that’s what makes me different than most developers. I’m not married to my code, it isn’t a representation of me now it’s a representation of a particular set of circumstances.

That said, if I build something completely awful that is a reflection on me. If I do it three times over, I should probably attempt to enhance my development skills. I shouldn’t put the work up as my pride and joy. I’ve written some pretty awful stories and I’ve written some pretty awful code. I look back on my first web application, my first large scale Drupal module, and my first large Drupal applications. They aren’t pretty, I would have built them very differently now. That happens though. That’s part of the growth process.
