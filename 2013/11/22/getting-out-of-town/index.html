Getting Out Of Town Life

November 22, 2013

Sometimes getting out of town is the best thing to do. It adds perspective and has a special way of reseting ones mind. There is a bonus in this trip as well, I get to see a couple of developers I haven’t seen in a while.

Not too long ago I made the realization that New York City might not be a good fit for me. There are plenty of great things going on here, a bunch of startups, great employment opportunities, and I’ve made some awesome friends here. The main problem is that it doesn’t fit well with my personality. I enjoy being laid back and taking things as they come. I dislike hurrying up and waiting, which is a near constant in this city. Perhaps I haven’t found the place in the city with an attitude that’s more “move quickly” and less “rush, rush, rush”1. As has been said many times to me, I can always come back.

The main problem is that I’m not inspired by this city, it was a safe move for me. How can I expect myself to push boundaries in my career if I can’t push boundaries concerning my choice of place to live. My goal is to move to the West Coast. If that doesn’t work, it’s off to somewhere new until I find a place that’s the best fit possible. Not perfect, just the least sucky.

That is partly the reason for my trip out of town this weekend. I’m trying to get out and explore the world. I think everyone should travel if they can, especially if you have a career in web development where it’s easier to find a job that allows working remotely. Even if without a remote job, one with nice vacation time works as well.

Networking

Exploring other cities outside one’s own also gives opportunities to network. There’s no need to wait for a giant conference. Conference are great, but sometimes a trip to see a couple of friends in the industry can be a less costly and effective way to accomplish the same task. You can also spend a little bit of time meeting some of their contacts in a much less overwhelming environment. There are also times when just hanging out with a couple of people is exactly what’s needed.

Exploring other cities has the nice side effect of seeing how other places are similar and different from the one you inhabit. I traveled quite a bit as a child, but I haven’t been anywhere outside of the United States (sans a couple trips to Canada). I plan on going to Europe, I would like to experience what it’s like outside of the United States. That said, my goals when traveling are different from the norm. If I’m getting out of town, I want to relax and have a fun time exploring places. That should be read “I don’t want to book every second of every day doing something and I especially don’t want to be near any tourist attractions”.

Projects with Friends

Distributed projects are the soul of the open source community. It wouldn’t work if everyone had to be in a room or building together to build software. That said, having a developer LAN party every once in a while isn’t a bad idea. Instead of running around to tourist attractions I would rather find a chill cafe and initiate a sprint. Especially since I’m going to Boston and I don’t eat seafood. Having a relaxing weekend with a couple of friends that ends with having a shiny new piece of code is always welcome.

Even with all the wonderful technology we have, like join.me, Google plus hangouts, Skype, GitHub, and IRC, nothing beats some quality face to face pair programming time. From what I’ve heard, there are people who believe pair programming is a waste of time because it takes two developers. I’ve also heard that code reviews are also a waste of resources. I can’t speak to code reviews much, but I’ve had phenomenal pair programming experiences. I’ve been able to solve major system bugs using it. I have faith the use of it prevents a plethora of bugs as well.

Seriously though, is there really a need to have an excuse to visit your friends and have fun doing what you love in a city that isn’t your own? I think not.

1: Yes, those are two different things. Moving quickly involves having a fast face but still taking the proper time to achieve a task. Rushing usually comes with awful compromises and from my experience is caused by financial strains.
