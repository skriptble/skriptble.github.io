Nodejs Authentication Meetups

November 18, 2013

Even though I’m not focusing on NodeJS at the moment, it is part of my core (via Javascript). When a meetup about authentication for NodeJS popped up in the suggested meetups I might want to attend I signed up right away. So here are some notes I gathered over the course of the meetup.

I’ve used NodeJS before. I understand how to build an application with it and the asynchronous nature of the framework. There are several applications out there using NodeJS in production, even though it doesn’t have that shiny 1.0 release yet. It feels as though NodeJS is a nice fit between the functional nature of Erlang and the procedural and object oriented feel of PHP.

The Meetup

There is nothing more encouraging than a group of excited developers talking about something they all love. I had a rather negative experience with the PHP meetup in town, but this meetup was energetic and the topic was engaging. The presenter did well, he wasn’t an ace NodeJS developer, but I think that brings a certain level of relief to a crowd. When there’s an ace developer presenting, sometimes they move too fast or it’s difficult to understand the concepts. The assume knowledge that comes as a given to them but is foreign to others. That wasn’t my experience at all.

The topic was authentication, and the presenter brought up and interesting topic: people do it wrong, a lot. The examples for attempting to do it correctly are scattered in a messy pile with the examples of wrong. Most authentication systems work like magic to me. I don’t general think about them and leave it to the framework I’m using to handle it.

There are simple things one can do to protect their users though. I think one of them is something that I run into quite often when logging into servers: passphrases. When I SSH into a remote server to do work or develop, I don’t use a password. I have an SSH public private key pair with a passphrase. The use of a phrase instead of a password is more secure by itself, the extra layer of public private keypair is the extra layer of protection that makes it awesome.

I believe this has been floating around on the internet for a while. Using a sentence with spaces instead of a password. Sure, the usage of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and punctuation makes a password more secure, but having a 30 character long passphrase with uppercase, lowercase, spaces, numbers, and punctuation is much better.

Imagine a world in which everyone’s authentication relied on a 35 character long sentence. Perhaps with a mobile app, you log in with it once, and then you assign a numeric code to unlock just that app. Or use phone or email for two factor authentication. It’s time to stop using the “please enter a password between 5 and 14 characters in length” systems and update to something more secure and easier for regular people to remember.

Aside from that revelation, I networked with some people. Exchanged some business cards, and I’ve ultimately decided to continue attending events like these. Being surrounded by a bunch of motivated, excited developers will definitely help advance my journey to mastering my core. The enthusiasm I received from the people I networked with, and just being able to talk at the same level was uplifting. I even managed to explain a concept to someone that they had never heard of. This is definitely a group I need to be a part of.
