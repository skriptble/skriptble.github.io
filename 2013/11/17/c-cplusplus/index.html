C & C++ The Core Languages

November 17, 2013

I am on a roll with discounts lately. It almost feels like I picked the perfect time to choose the core languages I want to learn. Or there are always deals and now I’m just utilizing them. Or it’s the holiday season so everything is on sale. It doesn’t really matter the reason, I got a hefty 40% discount on 2 books I just bought.

C++ Primer

Not to be confused with C++ Primer Plus. From my research this is one of the best books to learn C++. The only downside is it’s a heavyweight, coming in at just under 1,000 pages. As much as I love printed books, there was no way I was going to carry that thing anywhere, it would double the load I already carry with me. I’m realizing that theory and non-coding books are great for print, but trying to type the code in from a printed book while trying to keep it open and not make a mistake is a bit too much to tackle. My terminal is configured so it’s in drop down from the top visor mode. It’s also partially transparent, which means I can read text through it.

This didn’t matter much before, because the only way I could read an iBook was on my iPad or iPhone. Reading the PDF versions was annoying because it doesn’t sync the pages across my devices.1 With the release of iBooks on OS X that problem is now solved. In my quest to learn Puppet, I am taking full advantage of this. I’m making speedy progress through the book, and part of that I can attribute to being able to look at a single screen to do the examples and read the text. I am excited to read this book, and learn a lower level language. Perhaps along the way I can learn to write a PHP extension or two.

C Primer Plus

Unlike C++ Primer Plus, this book has great reviews, and while I’m not a complete new comer to C I think I need to start in the beginner realm. I took a chance and pre-ordered the new edition which is set to be released in early December. While this book also comes in at over 1,000 pages I’m ready to get an in depth look at one of core languages of computing today. As I traverse to deeper levels of programming and application development, my lack of understanding in areas such as memory management or input output operations is holding me back.

Since part of my core are two functional languages I believe knowing this base language will help me understand more why I would choose one of those languages over C. Currently I have a number of other resources that tell me I should learn Go and Erlang for various reasons, but Google and Stackoverflow won’t be able to tell me which projects I should use a functional language over an object oriented or procedural language on.

I’ve found it difficult to use online resources to learn both (C and C++) of these languages. Most online resources are dated or don’t give me the theory I need to understand why I’m writing this code. If I don’t understand something, I won’t be able to use it later on. It won’t stick well and I’ll wind up doing it the wrong or a much more inefficient way.

Overall, I’m excited to have added these two books to my collection. I’ve been eyeing them for a while, and once again discounts have given me the perfect opportunity to buy them at a steal.

1: Yes I do realize how much of a ‘First World Problem’ this is. My eBooks don’t sync their pages from my iPhone and iPad to the PDF’s on my computer. In reality, I’m not even sure if the syncing works between OSX iBooks and iOS iBooks.
