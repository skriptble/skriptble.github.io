Full Stack Engineering Meetups

November 26, 2013

Another day another Meetup. While I was fully expecting this Meetup to be like the rest, I was pleasantly surprised at the quality and information of this one. The quality of the informative post-Meetup drinks session was also surprising. Even though it was tough getting to the Meetup, I find myself happy that I decided to take the trip.

The Meetup

I have given Apple credit for Apple maps before, but one of the largest blunders I’ve seen is the Meetup app combined with Apple maps. Unfortunately, Apple maps doesn’t know how to parse out the helpful information (like which streets a building is in between) from the actual address, leading to absolutely bizarre addresses. The flavor today was pointing toward midtown instead of Tribecca. Oops. This unfortunate snafu resulted in my being half an hour late to the meetup. I missed part of the first presentation, however, the Meetup was still well worth it.

The topics discussed weren’t the normal type of information that I usually hear at Meetups. I knew almost instantly that this Meetup was different. From what I understand the concept of a Full Stack Engineer is new, but I think it’s the description that I’ve been looking for to explain my need to work on multiple layers of the stack.

Some of the interesting tibits of information I came away with were staggered deployments, reducing the size of the technology stack being used, and nifty tools like DTrace. While the technologies discussed are fascinating, I found the post Meetup drink sessions equally engaging. Some of the people I met are definitely the types of people that I’ve been looking for.

The most prominent revelation I had it something that has been bothering me for many months now. I remember about 6 months ago I was feeling down on Drupal due to my coworkers requests that I didn’t know how to solve. Eventually I dove into development more and discovered how to fulfill these request and was once again excited about using Drupal, but there was that part of me that was constantly fighting against it. Part of me that realized there are some large flaws in Drupal that aren’t being properly solved.

The Evolution

For a while I’ve been thinking about how amazing Drupal 8 will be and how it will solve some large problems. Thanks to someone I met at this meetup, my suspicions of how much Drupal 8 fixes have been reaffirmed. I knew there would still be problems with Drupal, I knew there would be things that didn’t function as well, and I knew our team would still need custom processes. What I didn’t realize was the core thing that makes Drupal so awesome also makes Drupal not the technology that I see myself using as much in the future.

Don’t get me wrong, Drupal is awesome. It’s a powerful system that can handle almost any task that’s thrown at it, but it has many shortcomings. As we build more complex projects at work, these shortcomings are becoming apparent. It is becoming difficult to continue to use systems like Drupal (or any of the traditional content management systems) when what we’re building is a hybrid web app website.

Drupal is geared toward a certain demographic of web developers and designers. The same goes for other tools like Wordpress and any tool. The problem I continually encounter is that Drupal doesn’t feel like quite the right fit for the production projects I’ve been building. Drupal functions very well for the prototypes we build, but complex systems feel overly complex at many levels, and some of reasons why I felt the need to continue using Drupal stem from inadequacies in my own understanding of engineering.

Following In Footsteps: A pre-evolution tale

I never intended on becoming an engineer. Both of my parents went to college and studied computer science. My dad got a masters in it. Growing up it was a constant at family gatherings, especially as I got closer to college, that someone would ask what I was going to go to college for. It was always be followed with an immediate “Computer Science like your dad?”, to which I would respond with various answers depending on the year, but the most resounding answer was “No, I’m going to be an author. I’m going to school for writing”. I did not want to follow in my dad’s footsteps, I was a creative and my dad always told me to take advantage of that1.

I couldn’t escape it though. I remember being a young preteen and getting book son how to build a blog. I still have my first programming book, and even though it’s very much beat up, I still cherish it. QBasic was the first programming language I learned and stopping myself from that point was moot, I was hooked. It wasn’t until college that I actually understand how to solve problems with programming. I took 2 courses in high school, one on C and the other a continuation of the first in C++.

I’ll write more about how I wound up as a software engineer at another point, but what I’ve come to realize is that resist as I might, I was heading toward being a Full Stack Engineer. I tried to focus on just the programming language layer, and not the database. Then I had to start learning how databases worked, which lead to optimizing my code, which lead to caching, which lead to custom server implementations, which lead to learning Linux, which lead to bare metal. It wasn’t as synchronous as that, but that’s essentially how it happened.

I picked up a book on Making Embedded Systems a few weeks ago, and I realized that I had done the exact opposite of what I said: I had followed in my dad’s footsteps. Today I realized that while I am doing the same types of things as my dad, I haven’t violated that creative determination I made. During the post Meetup drink session, I found myself with a group of guys talking heavily about theory, mostly mathematical and database theory, but theory nonetheless. I’ve had a constant problem finding a place where I felt I belonged. During the conversation, the ease which people switched between theory and concrete topics resonated with the way in which my mind functions. I realized that while engineering is usually seen as extremely technical it is also extremely creative.

At the end of the day I think I’ve found something important on multiple levels. While it was only one person who I talked in depth with Drupal about, his words were the encouragement I needed to realize that my thoughts and concerns were valid. That my uneasiness with using Drupal for so much wasn’t just me and that it actually validated. I, like many Drupalist, put Drupal and companies like Acquia on a pedestal. I try hard not to idealize things. I use Ubuntu, but it isn’t the best Linux out there, and MongoDB is the best database, PHP isn’t the best language, and Macs are not the best development environment. For some reason, I felt for a long time that Drupal was the best web platform. It isn’t. It’s a decent web platform, but my desire to be a Full Stack Engineer appears to directly conflict with the community push for how to use Drupal. I find PaaS an interesting concept, but I am beginning to realize that I will most likely only go as high as IaaS and might use PaaS for some things, but ultimately my desire to understand and tinker won’t work with PaaS unless I’m running the PaaS.

Ultimately, this Meetup opened my eyes to realizing that there isn’t something wrong with how I feel. I’m not doing anything wrong and simply wishing to have a deeper understanding doesn’t mean I’m doing things wrong. I am a builder. A creative engineer. I am a creative builder.

1: Thanks dad!
