Shipping And Small Steps

November 11, 2013

Initial perfection isn’t possible. The launch day is not the day an application or website will be perfected. This is why building minimum viable products and take smaller steps makes sense. Shipping is the most important thing, because the best product in the world won’t matter if it never sees the light of day.

“The first step is the hardest” An old and tired clich√©

That is wrong. While the first step is hard, it is certainly not that hardest and sometimes, it isn’t even hard. The first step is usually pretty easy, especially when starting a web application. Make a git repository, write an opening line of code, and make the initial commit. Code is cheap, especially compared to other disciplines like architecture or manufacturing. Those disciplines have physical things that are created. An issue with a building after it’s begun construction or product after some have been manufactured can become costly quickly. A bug in code doesn’t necessarily have the same ascent.

It’s import to get that thing being built into the hands of the users as soon as possible. Being first to market is important. The iPhone was the first phone of its type, and while the market is now filled with similar products, Apple had an advantage from getting there first. Same goes for applications.

Shipping first also sets the bar. Getting a product out there, even if its a crappy implementation, is better than no implementation. It’ll grow over time, it just needs that time to do so. Some features might not be necessary, and some that were overlooked might be that crucial thing users were looking for. Building applications in a vacuum is bad for the developer and the users.

The First Contract

One of the first contracts I landed as a developer was a reservation system. I had already built a similar system for an organization I was part of, so it felt like it would be easy. Looking back, what was built with the timeline involved was phenomenal. The main problem I continually ran into was a lack of response from the client on some of the features. We hadn’t established a minimum viable product, and there was no plan for how to address issues after the launch of the application. This lead to a headache for both myself and the client when trying to later implement the system.

If I was to rebuild that project I would get more involved in the client’s implementation and ensure stronger communication. A section of a book I’m currently reading (Without Their Permission: How the 21st Century will be made not managed) details how developers should get into the industry they are attempting to innovate. I had spent over a year handling the reservation and checkout of field and studio equipment, and then built a system facilitate that online. Even with that knowledge I still should have gone a step further with the second client. I should have picked up more shifts checking in and out equipment and handling the reservation of it. I should have done more exploratory exercises with the client, diving deeper into the underlying issues.

Thing is, I’m getting a second shot at it. While the system I built has seen its day and been retired for both the clients, the world still needs a better way of reserving stuff. I looked long and hard for a prepackaged solution to handle reserving things. Not a whole lot turned up. There were some implementations, but they were usually tied to a specific CMS or Framework, or they were standalone and cost a shitton of money.

It isn’t rocket science. It’s one of those problems that every implementation solves specifically for their exact requirements. They aren’t pluggable, the features of some are phenomenal, except they’re missing those one or two killer features that make the system work for the specific client.

I’ve been toying around with the idea of building an open source reservation system and API. The API is a big component. Making this system pluggable into a large number of platforms will only aid in easy of use. The other requirement is that it should have pluggable layers. Want to use Redis instead of MySQL? That should not force you to rebuild the front end. Want to add a open and closed hours for individual items or have them be part of a lab? Why not both?

I took the first step. I created a repository last night, and I plan on contributing something everyday. That’s the hard part, not starting, but taking continual small steps. One foot in front of the other, day by day. That’s how this will get done.
