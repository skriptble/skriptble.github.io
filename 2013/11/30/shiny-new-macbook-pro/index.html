Shiny New Macbook Pro

November 30, 2013

My current MacBook Pro has been a warrior. It’s a 2010 model, with a core 2 duo processor and 8GB of RAM. It has a SSD for the system drive (which uses the optical drive space) and a 500GB 7200RPM drive where the regular system drive goes. Sadly, it’s been through some rough times, include a 2 year period of just not working. It’s started disconnecting the 500GB Media Drive, the trackpad isn’t working as well, and Vagrant or any VM is nightmareisly slow. It’s time for a shiny new MacBook Pro.

Weight Matters

While my trusty 13” MacBook Pro is on it’s last legs, my 15” non-retina MacBook Pro is still going strong. Problem is the thing is heavy and cluttered with a mess of stuff. It’s running a bit light on RAM, has a spinning disk, and the display isn’t retina. That weight thing is the killer though. I live in New York City, so I spend most of my time getting around via walking or taking a subway. Having an extra couple pounds on my back is not the ideal situation.

Plus, separation of concerns: I use the 15” MacBook Pro for photography and video editing. I use my current 13” MacBook Pro for web development. While possible, I would rather not overload a computer with too many things1.

I’ve been thinking about getting a new computer for a while, so it isn’t as if I just decided spontaneously to buy one. Just like the core languages I choose to learn, this decision came from many months of planning and realizing that my computer wasn’t going to last forever.

Forward Thinking

Another reason I choose to buy new instead of fix up the old and dual purpose is that starting new allows me to plan out how I’m going to set up my computer. While this is another circumstance where maintenance could be better than starting new, it isn’t so easy. I haven’t used my photog and video MacBook Pro for web development in a few years. The software is old, the development systems I used are completely different now, and there are several programs on the machine that take up space and bog down the system2. Overall buying new allows me to get the specs I need, without the bulk I don’t.

During the Full Stack Engineering Meetup I went to a few days ago, one of the presenters said that you should use tools that do most of what you want and no more. Meaning that you shouldn’t use something that does way more than you need because it goes to waste.

When I purchased my photog and video MacBook Pro, I was thinking about video editing. I have a graphics card that eats of the battery, not a ton of RAM, a large amount of storage space, and a powerful CPU. I was also thinking I would be close to a power source, mainly because video editing eats up the battery. This is major overkill for a web development computer. I work in the terminal, so a graphics card isn’t on the top of my mind. I need RAM for the virtual machines and databases I run. I don’t need much storage space, most of my files measure in the kilobytes or megabytes, not gigabytes like video files. While I am getting into concurrent programming, I need more CPUs not more powerful ones. Finally, I live in New York City and cafes like not having power available3. If I don’t want to be tied to my apartment or office, I need a long lasting battery life. I can get a couple hours out of my WebDev MacBook Pro right now but that doesn’t give much time to work on projects. Especially on a weekend day.

What Shiny I Got

So I got the shiny MacBook Pro with retina display. I originally wanted one with 256GB of solid state storage, but they only had the configuration I wanted with 512GB of solid state storage. I could have ordered it and had it shipped, but that’s another two or more weeks. Trying to figure out where I’ll be to receive the machine and having to deal with a rapidly dying WebDev MacBook Pro (the Media Drive just disconnected again so it only allows reads not writes) I just can’t afford to deal with the frustration.

At the end of the day, I’m very happy that I was able to get everything in order to purchase a new computer so quickly. With my tight timeline for goals, I can’t afford to lose weeks of time getting back on my feet from a computer failure. Not losing progress is well worth the extra $200.
